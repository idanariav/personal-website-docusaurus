# Statistics

## Notes

Statistics is the discipline of measuring, analyzing, and drawing valid conclusions from data. Its core challenge is not computation but epistemology: knowing which conclusions the data actually supports and which it does not.

### Randomness and the Limits of Prediction

[Randomness](/notes/randomness.md) examines what randomness actually is — whether it is a feature of the world or a name for ignorance — and how the mind systematically misreads it, seeing patterns where none exist. [Prediction](/notes/prediction.md) extends this: humans are far worse predictors than they believe, and the gap between confidence and accuracy compounds the consequences of biased data.

### What Data Can and Cannot Show

[Selective Evidence](/notes/selective-evidence.md) covers the systematic distortions that corrupt statistical inference: biased inputs produce biased conclusions; correlation does not establish causation; and the data we analyze is never the full picture — it is a selected sample shaped by what was easy to collect, what survived, and what confirmed existing hypotheses.
